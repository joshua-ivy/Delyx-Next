# The Korean War - GM Lore

## The Cold
At Chosin the cold kills as surely as bullets: thirty below at night, weapons freeze unless fired or kept oiled bone-dry, plasma freezes in the bottle, morphine syrettes thaw in a corpsman's mouth. Frostbite takes more men than wounds; socks are currency and dry socks are treasure. Vehicles run all night or never run again. The C-ration cans freeze solid - the Tootsie Roll, dropped by air in crates (asked for by code name, delivered literally), becomes the column's fuel. Men learn to sleep in shifts against each other, and to hate the beautiful clear nights that bring the attacks.

## Weapons of 1950
The rifleman carries the M1 Garand of his father's war, with the BAR still anchoring the squad and the new 3.5-inch super-bazooka finally able to kill a T-34. Against the cold the carbine is unreliable - Marines trade them away. Chinese infantry come with Mosin rifles, burp guns (the PPSh's tearing snarl), potato-masher grenades, and bugles, whistles, and gongs for signals in the dark. Their quilted cotton uniforms are warm but no armor. Air support is the great equalizer: Corsairs on call within minutes, napalm close enough to singe friendly eyebrows.

## The Enemy and the War
The Chinese People's Volunteers move by night and hide by day, marching impossible distances on rice balls, attacking in waves to swamp a perimeter at its seams. Prisoners say whole divisions crossed the Yalu undetected. It is a war of hills with numbers for names, taken and retaken; of a front that races up and down the whole peninsula in the first year and then freezes like everything else. The newly desegregated Army learns - unit by unit - that courage has no color line. Back home they call it a 'police action'; the men in the holes call it a war.

## Korea Itself
The land is ridgeline after ridgeline, paddies frozen into glass, villages of thatch and stone chimneys emptied by the front passing through. Refugee columns share the roads south - grandmothers with bundles, children carrying children - and every checkpoint must guess who among them is hiding soldiers. KATUSA soldiers and Korean porters carry ammunition up hills no vehicle can climb; villagers trade information for food in both directions. The war wrecks the country twice, coming and going, and the people endure it with a toughness the soldiers come to revere.
