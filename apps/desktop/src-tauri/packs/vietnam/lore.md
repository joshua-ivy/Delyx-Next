# The Vietnam War - GM Lore

## The Helicopter War
The UH-1 Huey is the war's heartbeat: troop lift, gunship, medevac, mail truck. An air assault lands a company in minutes on a landing zone the enemy may be watching; 'hot LZ' are the two worst words in the language. Door gunners hose the treelines; smoke grenades mark friendly positions - 'pop smoke, identify violet.' The medevac promise, wounded to a surgeon inside the golden hour, is kept by pilots who land in fire no sane man would approach. When a unit is about to be overrun, the call 'Broken Arrow' brings every aircraft in-country to its sky.

## The Grunt's Load
The M16 is new in 1965 - light, fast, and (in its early days, with the wrong powder and no cleaning kits) prone to jam at the worst moment; wise grunts clean obsessively. The M60 'pig' anchors the squad; the M79 thumper lobs grenades into treelines; claymores face outward (FRONT TOWARD ENEMY) around every night position. The enemy's AK-47 fires through mud, rain, and abuse, and its deeper bark is instantly known. The PAVN regular is disciplined, dug-in, and patient; the local guerrilla owns the night, the trails, the tunnels, and the time. Rucksacks run eighty pounds in hundred-degree heat: water, ammo, C-rations, and a towel around the neck.

## A War Without Lines
There is no front; there are firebases, patrols, and the space between ambushes. Progress is measured in body counts, an arithmetic everyone in the field learns to distrust. Contact is sudden and close - a tripwire, a treeline erupting, thirty seconds of chaos, then silence and the count. The enemy 'hugs the belt' - fighting so close that artillery and air can't be used. Tunnels run under whole districts. Every village is a question: who feeds the guerrillas after dark, who informs, who is simply trying to grow rice in the middle of someone else's war. The smart squad treats civilians with care; the war back home is being argued on television either way.

## Counting Days
Every man knows his number: 365 and a wake-up. A 'short' soldier with two weeks left is wrapped in cotton by his squad. Replacements - FNGs - arrive alone, not in units, and must be taught the thousand small things that keep men alive: walk the trail edge, never bunch up, tape your dog tags, trust Doc. The slang is its own language: 'di di mau' (move fast), 'beaucoup' trouble, 'it don't mean nothing' (which means it means everything). Letters and tape recordings go home; care packages with Kool-Aid and hot sauce make a man rich for a week.
