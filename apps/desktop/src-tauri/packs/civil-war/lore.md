# The American Civil War - GM Lore

## Rifles and the Old Tactics
The infantryman carries a rifled musket - the Springfield Model 1861 or the British Enfield - a muzzle-loader firing the Minie ball, deadly at three hundred yards. The tactics, though, are older than the rifle: shoulder-to-shoulder lines, volleys, and bayonet charges across open ground. That mismatch is why the casualty lists are unthinkable. Three shots a minute standing, fewer lying down. Artillery fires solid shot, shell, and - at close range - canister, which turns a cannon into a giant shotgun. Cavalry scouts and screens; it rarely charges infantry who hold steady.

## The Soldier's Day
A day is mostly marching: sixteen miles on good roads, dust or mud, blanket roll over one shoulder. Rations are hardtack ('teeth-dullers'), salt pork, and coffee - coffee is sacred, traded across picket lines for Southern tobacco when officers aren't looking. Disease kills two soldiers for every one lost in battle: typhoid, dysentery, measles in the new regiments. Letters and hometown newspapers are everything; a regiment is a neighborhood in uniform, and a bad afternoon can erase a town's young men.

## Why They Fight
The war is about slavery - secession's own declarations said so - but the man in the ranks carries his own reasons besides: Union, home, his messmates, the bounty that fed his family, or the simple fact that his county raised a regiment and he was seventeen. After September 1862 the Emancipation Proclamation makes the Union army an army of liberation wherever it marches, and by war's end nearly two hundred thousand Black soldiers - many freshly escaped from slavery, like the USCT regiments - fight in blue. Confederate ranks hold true believers, conscripts, and boys following cousins; let them be people, not arguments.

## Signals, Spies, and Lost Orders
Both armies read each other's newspapers, tap telegraph lines, and watch signal flags on mountaintops (the wig-wag code is brand new). Civilians pass word across the lines - enslaved people consistently bring the Union its best intelligence, at enormous personal risk. Orders travel by mounted courier and go astray: the entire Maryland campaign turns on a mislaid copy of Lee's Special Order 191. Maps are bad; armies blunder into each other at crossroads towns, which is how a shoe-rumor town like Gettysburg becomes a battlefield.
