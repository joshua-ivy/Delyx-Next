# The American Revolution - GM Lore

## Muskets and Powder
The militiaman carries a smoothbore flintlock musket - a Brown Bess if taken from the British, a French Charleville later in the war, or grandfather's fowling piece. Accurate to maybe fifty yards, three shots a minute for a practiced hand, useless in rain when the powder pan gets wet. Loading is a ritual: cartridge bitten open, powder, ball, ramrod. Bayonets decide close fights, and militia rarely have them - which is why militia break when Regulars charge. Rifles shoot far but load slow and mount no bayonet. Powder is precious everywhere; whole campaigns turn on a few barrels.

## Militia and Regulars
The minuteman is a farmer who drills on the green a few days a year; the British Regular is a professional who has drilled for years. In open field, line against line, the Regular wins. So the militiaman's war is walls, trees, and fences - aimed fire and quick feet, which the British call dishonorable and learn to fear on the road back from Concord. The Continental Army that Washington builds is something between the two: ragged, underpaid, and by Valley Forge's end, drilled by Baron von Steuben into an army that can stand in line and trade volleys.

## A Divided Country
This is a civil war as much as a revolution. Perhaps a third of neighbors are Patriot, a third Loyalist, a third praying it passes by. Loyalists inform, smuggle, and raise regiments of their own; Patriot committees tar-and-feather, confiscate, and watch the roads. Spies are everywhere - powder hidden under hay, messages in hollow buttons, women carrying word through checkpoints because soldiers rarely search them. Choosing a side can cost a farm, a church pew, a brother.

## Daily Life of 1775
News travels at the speed of a good horse - a rider like Paul Revere or William Dawes, or the chain of alarm bells and signal guns from town to town. Money is a mess of shillings, Spanish dollars, and soon worthless Continental paper. Medicine is grim: a wound means the surgeon's probe, and smallpox kills more soldiers than the British do (Washington eventually orders inoculation). Taverns are the newspapers, recruiting stations, and parliaments of the countryside.
