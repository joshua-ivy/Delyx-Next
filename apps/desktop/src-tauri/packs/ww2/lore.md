# World War II (ETO) - GM Lore

## The Rifleman's Tools
The M1 Garand is the squad's backbone: semi-automatic, eight-round clip that ejects with a famous ping, reliable in sand and mud. The BAR gives the squad automatic fire; the Thompson goes to NCOs and tankers; the bazooka can kill a tank if you're brave and close and aim for the side or rear. Grenades are the Mk 2 pineapple. The German MG42 fires so fast it sounds like ripping cloth - squads learn to move while it changes barrels. The 88 is the gun everyone fears; mortars cause more casualties than anything else. K-rations keep you alive and make you hate breakfast.

## The Bocage
Normandy behind the beaches is hedgerow country: small fields walled by earthen banks topped with dense brush, each one a ready-made fortress. Tanks climbing a hedgerow show their soft bellies; infantry crossing a field gap walk into pre-sighted machine guns. The fight is field by field, gate by gate, with grenades and point-blank armor until someone welds steel teeth onto Shermans (the Rhino tanks, invented by a sergeant) to bust through the banks. Progress is measured in hundreds of yards and the rifle companies pay for every one.

## The Squad and the Army Behind It
A rifle squad is twelve men who carry each other's letters and know each other's snoring. Replacements arrive young and nameless and either learn fast or don't last; good squads adopt them anyway. Behind the rifleman stands the richest logistics machine in history - gasoline, ammunition, Spam, and mail that mostly arrives - and the Red Ball Express trucking columns, largely driven by Black soldiers in a segregated army fighting for freedoms not all of them are given at home. Combat fatigue is real; even the bravest have a breaking point, and the wise sergeant rotates men before they reach it.

## Occupied France
Civilians live among the fighting: farm families who hide in cellars and offer cider and eggs to liberators, villages with German garrisons and informers, and the Resistance - the Maquis - cutting wires, marking targets, and guiding patrols. Most French civilians have spent four years under occupation: rationing, curfews, requisitions, neighbors deported. Liberation is church bells, wine from buried bottles, and sometimes a sniper left behind in the steeple. Paratroopers scattered in the night still walk in from the marshes days later, fighting with whoever finds them.
